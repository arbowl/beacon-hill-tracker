Massachusetts Legislative Analysis · 194th General Court

No Vote Required

Nine committees invoked a procedural timeout to send 704 bills to study without a recorded vote — an average of 171 days after each bill's deadline had already passed

By Drew Bowler, Beacon Hill Compliance Tracker  ·  Data through April 4, 2026

When a Massachusetts legislative committee sends a bill to study, it ordinarily requires a committee vote. But House Rule 27 contains a provision that waives that requirement once a bill's reporting deadline has expired: the matter is deemed to have "timed out," and no poll of committee members is required. Nine Joint Committees in the 194th General Court used this provision — filing 704 bills to study under the timeout mechanism, with an average of 171 days elapsed since each bill's individual deadline had already passed.

Every bill in this analysis carries the same two-part record. On the bill's action history page, the entry reads: "Accompanied a study order, see H5xxx (under House Rule 27)." On the committee vote tab, the entry reads: "Under the provisions of House Rule 27, matter timed out so no poll is required."

The timeout provision in House Rule 27 exists because the Legislature recognized that committees sometimes fail to act within their allotted window — and that there needs to be a mechanism to formally close out those bills. What this analysis documents is the lag between when that timeout technically occurred (the bill's individual deadline) and when committees filed the paperwork to record it.

How the Mechanism Works

Under House Rule 27, the House chair of each Joint Committee is required to file a final report on a bill within 60 days of it being heard — extendable to 90 days with a formal extension order. Once that window closes without action, the rule provides that the matter may be accompanied by a study order without a committee vote. The committee vote tab for any such bill will state that the "matter timed out so no poll is required."

This provision is procedurally distinct from a standard study order, in which the committee convenes, takes a recorded vote, and files the outcome. Under the timeout mechanism, no such vote occurs. The decision to send the bill to study is made without a formal recorded action by committee members — which means there is no public record of how individual legislators voted, or whether they objected.

Every one of the 704 bills in this analysis has a committee vote tab that reads the same way: "Under the provisions of House Rule 27, matter timed out so no poll is required."

Methodology

This analysis is based on publicly available records from the Massachusetts Legislature's official website, malegislature.gov. For each Joint Committee active in the 194th General Court, the tracker collected bill hearing records, bill status pages, and the legislative action history and committee vote records posted for each individual bill.

Identifying HR27 timeout filings. When a bill is sent to study under the House Rule 27 timeout mechanism, two records are created on malegislature.gov. First, the bill's action history includes an entry reading "Accompanied a study order, see H5xxx (under House Rule 27)." Second, the bill's Committee Vote tab includes the text "Under the provisions of House Rule 27, matter timed out so no poll is required." This analysis identifies bills using the first record — specifically, the presence of the phrase "under House Rule 27" within the study order action text. This text is consistent and distinctive across all instances found in the dataset.

Identifying the deadline. Each bill's reporting deadline was computed from the date the bill was formally heard by its committee. Under House Rule 27, a committee has 60 days from the hearing date to make final report; this window may be extended to 90 days where a formal extension order was posted to the Legislature's website. The resulting figure is referred to as the "effective deadline." Senate bills carry no separate Senate rule deadline and fall back to Joint Rule 10, which sets the first Wednesday in December as the reporting deadline for bills heard before October 1, or 60 days for bills heard after October 1.

The comparison. A bill is included in this analysis if it meets two conditions: (a) its action history contains "under House Rule 27" in the study order text, and (b) the date of that study order filing is later than the bill's effective deadline. Every bill meeting condition (a) in the 194th General Court dataset also meets condition (b) — there were no HR27 timeout filings recorded on or before the bill's individual deadline.

Scope. The analysis covers Joint Committees of the 194th General Court (2025–2026). Data was collected through April 4, 2026; the total of 704 bills should be understood as a floor, not a final count, as the session's reporting windows had not fully closed on that date.

Assumptions & Limitations
  • Text matching. This analysis identifies HR27 timeout filings by the presence of "under House Rule 27" in the study order action text. If any such filings exist that use different language or carry no parenthetical notation, they would not be captured here. However, the Legislature's use of this language is consistent across all 704 instances found, and it corresponds directly to the committee vote tab language on the same bills.
  • Hearing date as the clock start. If a bill was heard but the hearing was not posted to the website, or was posted with an incorrect date, the computed deadline may differ from the actual one. All hearing dates are sourced from the Legislature's own published records.
  • Extension orders. Extensions are applied only where a formal extension order was publicly posted. Unposted extensions are not reflected in the effective deadline.
  • No inference about legislative intent. This analysis documents a procedural pattern — bills accompanied by a study order under HR27's timeout provision, filed after each bill's deadline. It does not draw conclusions about whether individual legislators agreed with the outcome, were aware of it, or would have voted differently had a poll been taken.
  • Session scope. The 194th General Court convened in January 2025. Bills from prior sessions are not included.

The full dataset underlying this analysis, including individual bill IDs, committee assignments, hearing dates, effective deadlines, study order dates, and the text of both the action history and committee vote tab entries, is maintained by the Beacon Hill Compliance Tracker and is available upon request.
