Member Voting Patterns in Committee

How often do legislators vote independently?

Analysis of 20,471 committee votes • 194th General Court through February 2026

When Massachusetts legislative committees take recorded votes, members vote with their committee chair's recommendation 87% of the time. But beneath that aggregate figure lies substantial variation by chamber and party.

This analysis examines how Massachusetts legislators vote in committee—specifically, whether members exercise independent judgment or typically defer to committee leadership. The data reveals consistent patterns across chambers and parties.

Senate Republicans dissent on nearly half of all votes, while House Democrats dissent on fewer than one in 200. Members in the minority party are substantially more likely to vote against their committee chair's recommendation than those in the majority.

Reserve Rights votes—a procedural way to signal disagreement without casting a formal Nay—account for 43% of all Senate Republican votes, compared to less than 2% for Senate Democrats.

Favorable Reports vs. Study Orders

When committees send bills to "study"—effectively ending their consideration—members are more likely to dissent than when reporting bills favorably. This pattern holds across all groups:

Members are roughly twice as likely to dissent when bills are sent to study. For Senate Republicans, the dissent rate on study orders reaches 70.5%—more than double their rate on favorable reports.

Among Joint Committees, 28.8% of report-out actions have no posted vote record. Bills sent to study were less likely to have recorded votes (59%) than bills reported favorably (78%), suggesting committees are less inclined to create public voting records when the outcome is negative.

This vote analysis covers only bills where committees did post votes—representing 71.2% of all committee report-out actions in the 194th General Court through February 2026.

What This Means

The 87% overall alignment rate indicates that committee votes typically reflect consensus rather than contested deliberation. However, this aggregate figure masks significant variation:

Majority-party members (Democrats in both chambers) vote with their chairs more than 96% of the time. Minority-party members show substantially more independence, particularly in the Senate where Republicans dissent on nearly half of all votes.

The increased dissent on study orders—bills being effectively killed—suggests members are more willing to register disagreement when bills are being buried than when they're advancing.

Methodology

Analysis covers 20,471 individual member votes on bills with terminal committee outcomes (favorable report or study order) in Joint Committees of the 194th General Court, based on vote documents posted to malegislature.gov through February 20, 2026.

Committee chairs are excluded from all calculations. Party affiliations reflect member data from October 2025. Parse artifacts (garbled text from PDF documents, representing 2.6% of raw records) were identified and excluded. Voice votes and unanimous consent actions leave no individual record and are not captured in this analysis.

"Dissent" includes both Adverse (Nay) votes and Reserve Rights (Present) votes. "Favorable" refers to Yea votes supporting the chair's motion. No Action votes (5.3% of total) are counted separately.
